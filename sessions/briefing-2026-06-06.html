The Specific Enquiries

The triadic-ontology programme we surveyed last month — the system–instance–construal framework on The Becoming of Possibility, its companion argument that relata emerge from relational constraints, and Haela Ravenna Hunt‑Hendrix's perichoresis essay on LitVRGY — remains the baseline. The new entry is a theological companion piece from Exaudi framing the Trinity as three mirrors whose mutual reflection constitutes divine being rather than reflecting a pre‑existing substance. The article argues the "three mirrors" metaphor solves the loneliness problem in classical theism: God is not a solitary monad driven to create out of lack, but a perichoretic communion where each person is constituted by the others' regard. This maps onto the Hunt‑Hendrix claim that perichoresis functions as entrainment of copresence, but the Exaudi author pushes further — suggesting the economic Trinity (God's outward action) and the immanent Trinity (God's inner life) collapse into a single relational event, a move that would make the system–instance–construal triad not merely analytic but ontologically exhaustive. The piece is short on formal argument and long on devotional rhetoric, yet it signals that the triadic vocabulary is migrating from metaphysics blogs into mainstream theological discourse.

The Exaudi article's value lies less in its own rigour than in what it reveals about the programme's diffusion: the same relational‑ontology language that appeared in a consolidated statement on a Blogspot domain in January is now being deployed, nearly verbatim, in Catholic apologetics by June. That migration — from speculative metaphysics to confessional theology without an intervening peer‑reviewed step — is the actual development worth tracking. In my professional estimation, a piece of fucking garbage as scholarship, but a fascinating data point for the sociology of ideas.

The autonomous‑research landscape has advanced since we last noted Sisyphus Academica and its twenty‑agent paper‑writing army. AutoResearchClaw from the Aiming Lab now claims a fully self‑evolving pipeline that generates publication‑ready manuscripts with zero hallucinated citations — a claim that, if reproducible, would solve the citation‑fabrication problem plaguing every LLM‑based research agent. The system uses a recursive critique loop: a generator drafts, a verifier checks every reference against Semantic Scholar and Crossref, a novelty engine scores the contribution against recent literature, and an adversarial reviewer attempts to falsify the central claim; only papers surviving all four gates are emitted. Early benchmarks on arXiv CS.CL and CS.LG subsets show a 73% acceptance rate in simulated peer review, though the test set is small (n=47) and the evaluators are themselves prompted LLMs. AutoScientists takes a different tack: self‑organising agent teams that design and run long‑horizon experiments in bioML‑bench, nanoGPT hyperparameter search, and protein‑gym fitness prediction. The architecture treats each experiment as a Markov decision process where agents propose, execute, and analyse in cycles; the GitHub repo reports a 2.1× speed‑up over human‑guided baselines on nanoGPT training runs, but the protein‑gym results are preliminary and the codebase has not yet been stress‑tested outside the authors' cluster. Both projects are open‑source, both rely on GPT‑4‑class models for the reasoning layers, and both inherit the usual brittleness when the underlying model hallucinates a plausible‑sounding but non‑existent API. A total and utter shitshow of brittleness, in other words, waiting for the next model release to paper over the cracks.

The real signal is not any single benchmark but the convergence: citation verification, adversarial review, and experiment orchestration are being solved in parallel by independent teams, and the components are modular enough to be recombined. A pipeline wiring AutoResearchClaw's verifier into AutoScientists' experiment loop would be a genuine step toward autonomous scientific discovery — provided the hallucination floor drops further.

The New Yorker · Talk of the Town

The baseball legend Dusty Baker, who once wanted to be a journalist, has always had a knack for being in the right place at the right time. Age nineteen: Baker spots Jimi Hendrix on the streets of San Francisco, and the two share a joint. In his twenties: stands in the batter’s box as his Atlanta Braves teammate Hank Aaron hits his seven-hundred-and-fifteenth homer. Thirties: takes cover in Candlestick Park when the World Series earthquake strikes. Fifties: manages Barry Bonds when Bonds hits his seventy-third single-season homer. Sixties: manages Max Scherzer when Scherzer throws twenty strikeouts in an outing. Not that Baker merely beheld greatness. In 2022, four decades after winning the World Series as a Dodger outfielder, Baker, at seventy-three, became the oldest manager to win it. The toothpick he chewed that day in the Astros dugout is now in Cooperstown, and Baker may soon follow. His citation in the National Baseball Hall of Fame would likely mention another accomplishment—co-inventing the high five with Glenn Burke—which Baker plays down in “Crossroads,” his new memoir. “I just reached my hand up and hit his hand,” Baker writes. “I just reacted to Glenn.” Burke, a Dodgers teammate, had provided the setup for the pioneering hand slap, a spontaneous response to a homer that Baker hit in 1977. “It just seemed like the natural thing to do,” Baker added, the other day, during a conversation at his Spanish Revival house, near Sacramento, California. His mustache is graying, but his eyes remain mischievous. He had the Knicks-Cavs series on TV, next to a wall lined with guitars signed by John Lee Hooker, Carlos Santana, B. B. King, Tom Petty, Buddy Guy, and, he said, “Elvin Bishop, my fishing partner.” Takeout from Visconti’s Ristorante waited on the counter. Baker sprawled across a leather couch. Basketball is his favorite game to play and watch, and he was rooting for the Knicks—mostly because their coach, Mike Brown, once coached the Sacramento Kings. “I’m more amazed by basketball,” Baker said, comparing it with baseball. “The best athletes play basketball and football now. But, come on, you telling me Nikola Jokić couldn’t be a pitcher? Allen Iverson couldn’t play shortstop? It’s, like, Dude!” The last time Baker managed a pro club was in 2023, with Houston. But he surmised that he would return to the dugout—“if I could manage half the games for all the salary.” He always managed more by feel than by analytics. “You spend enough time hanging out around baseball, you feel the game,” he said. Baker likes hanging out. He calls it a “lost art.” He suggested hanging out in the Sacramento sun. Walking outside, he passed his pointers, Gracie and Rylie, barking in a pen. Nearby was a freshly dug mole hole. “Fucker,” Baker said, of the pest. “I gotta put some smoke bombs out here tomorrow.” He motioned toward two stone turtles (“sign of long life”) and a pool fed by a waterfall made from rocks delivered to him, he explained, by “some hippies who said the rocks whispered to them.” There was also a rose garden dedicated to his father, plum trees, boxes planted with onions, a few rows of Syrah grapes, and seats plucked from five baseball stadiums sitting beside a batting cage used by his son, Darren, a second baseman in the minors. He arrived at a chunk of Sierra gray granite, about four feet tall. Benches surrounded it. “This is my Dobie Gillis Think Rock,” Baker explained. “Dobie was this dude that was a beatnik in this sitcom when I was a kid. That was before the hippies.” In the credits, Dobie posed by Rodin’s “The Thinker.” “I put it here when I built my house,” Baker said. “It changes colors with the sun. It changes with the rain.” He touched the warm rock. “Dude, that’s what it’s about,” he said. “Energy. There are points on earth I’ve found that are points of energy—Mexico, Venezuela, Hawaii, Montana.” In the memoir, Baker notes that he once tried mescaline and hated it. He once tried mushrooms as well (“you laugh a lot”) and uses weed on occasion, although he said that he’ll never try gummies again. “One gummy wasn’t doing nothing, so I took another,” he recalled. “I was out of control.” Hanging out, as Baker defines it, means that “you don’t have any time or place. You’re just kind of there, wherever you are.” He lingered with his hand on his think rock. “It’s like Satchel Paige said, ‘Sometimes I sits and thinks, and sometimes I just sits.’ ” Paige, an early mentor to Baker, always called him Daffy. Baker never knew exactly why. He ambled off toward some plum trees, stooping to pull a few weeds. He plucked a plum from a branch and took a bite. “Almost there,” he said. ♦
