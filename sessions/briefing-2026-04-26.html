The first thing you notice, flying into LAX these days, is how green everything looks from above. Not the green of lawns—that particular shade of prosperity has been banned since the restrictions took effect—but the deeper, more complicated green of what happens when a city decides to let itself go wild. Along the 405, where the medians used to be mowed to golf-course precision, native sage and California poppies now grow in deliberate chaos. The Getty Center, which spent decades maintaining its manicured perfection, has become something closer to a Mediterranean hillside, all olive trees and rosemary gone feral.

"We're calling it 'rewilding,'" says Dr. Elena Morales, the urban ecologist who's become something of a celebrity in certain circles. She's sitting in her office at UCLA, surrounded by maps showing the transformation of Los Angeles from a city that imported water to one that harvests it. "The irony is that the crisis forced us to become what we always should have been." The numbers are staggering: residential water use down 74 percent in six months, but more remarkably, the city has actually added 2,300 acres of green space—just not the kind anyone expected.

In Silver Lake, where the hipsters used to compete over who could maintain the most perfect drought-tolerant garden, there's now a kind of reverse competition. The house with the most native bees wins. The apartment building with the best greywater system gets featured in design blogs. Even the Kardashians have gotten in on it—Kim posted a video yesterday of her new "harvest garden," which turns out to be mostly native milkweed and California buckwheat. "The butterflies love it," she says, sounding genuinely surprised.

The transformation extends beyond mere landscaping. Restaurants have become obsessed with "hyperlocal" cuisine—not the old farm-to-table movement, but something more radical. At Bestia, they're serving what they call "sidewalk salads": weeds harvested from the cracks in the pavement outside, dressed with olive oil from trees that were planted as ornamentals but now actually bear fruit. The Times food section ran a feature on "culinary foraging" that included recipes for dandelion greens and nasturtium pesto. Even the taco trucks have adapted—there's one on Alvarado that's famous for its purslane tacos.

But the most profound changes are the ones you can't see. The city's relationship with time has shifted. Without the constant pressure of maintaining appearances—watering, mowing, pruning—people seem to have discovered something that looks suspiciously like patience. In Echo Park, where the lake has been allowed to shrink to its natural size, neighbors have started organizing what they call "watching parties." Not watching TV, but watching the water. The herons that have returned to the reeds. The turtles that have emerged from wherever turtles go when they're not being watched.

"It's like we've all become amateur naturalists," says Marcus Chen, who used to be a talent agent but now runs something called the Los Angeles Rewilding Society out of his garage. "My daughter can identify fifteen different kinds of native bees. She's four." He's showing me his latest project: a map of every oak tree in Los Feliz, each one tagged with a QR code that links to its own Instagram account. "People send me photos of the trees changing through the seasons. It's like we're all learning to see again."

The economic implications are still sorting themselves out. Landscapers have become "rewilding consultants" and command higher fees than they ever did for mowing lawns. The city's water department, which used to be the villain in every story, has become an unlikely hero—its engineers have become celebrities on the lecture circuit, talking about how they redesigned an entire city's relationship with rain. Even the real estate market has adapted: properties with mature native gardens now command premiums over those with traditional landscaping.
