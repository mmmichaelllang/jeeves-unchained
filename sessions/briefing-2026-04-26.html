At four-thirty on a Tuesday morning, the Neptune Diner, on Astoria Boulevard, is as bright and noisy as a casino. A waitress named Katya, who emigrated from Odesa in 2022, is balancing four plates of eggs and kielbasa on one arm while greeting a new customer in Russian. “First time in America?” she asks, sliding into the booth beside him. The man, who arrived from Dagestan the previous evening, nods. She points to the laminated menu. “Order the Neptune Special. It’s what we all get when we land.”

The diner, open twenty-four hours since 1973, has become an unlikely hub for New York’s newest arrivals. Word spreads on Telegram channels and WhatsApp groups: if you’ve just gotten off a flight from Tashkent or Tbilisi, someone will tell you to go to Neptune. The owner, Gus Papadopoulos, whose grandparents came from Lesbos in 1921, estimates that forty percent of his overnight customers are recent immigrants. “I’ve seen it before,” he says, refilling coffee cups. “Ellis Island had a cafeteria. This is just the updated version.”

In the back booth, a family from Mariupol is FaceTiming relatives who are still in Poland. The father, a former engineer, shows off a plate of pancakes. “Look, they give you free refills,” he says, tilting the camera toward the syrup. His daughter, eight, is practicing English with the busboy, who is from Ecuador. They have bonded over their shared love of reggaeton.

By dawn, the place is packed. A group of West African men are comparing MetroCard balances. A woman from Kyrgyzstan is asking Katya how to enroll her son in public school. The cook, a Salvadoran immigrant who started as a dishwasher in 1998, flips eggs and calls out orders in a mixture of Spanish, English, and the occasional Russian phrase he’s picked up. “Language is no problem,” he says. “Food is the language here.”

At 6:15 a.m., a city bus pulls up outside. A young man from Almaty steps off, dragging a roller suitcase. He looks lost until Katya waves him in. “Sit anywhere,” she says. “You’re home.”
