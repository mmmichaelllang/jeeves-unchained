The first thing you notice about the new generation of AI pendants is that they’re not trying to be invisible. Unlike the failed “wearable cameras” of the 2010s, these devices—most no larger than a silver dollar—are designed to be seen. At the Soho pop-up for Friend, the latest entrant from former Apple engineer Avi Schiffmann, the pendants hang from neon cords like minimalist jewelry. “We’re not hiding the tech,” Schiffmann told me, gesturing at a display case of pastel prototypes. “We’re making it aspirational.”

The aspirational pitch is working. Since its soft launch last month, Friend has sold 40,000 units at $99 apiece, mostly to Gen Z users who post unboxing videos set to trending audio. The device records ambient audio in 30-second loops, transcribing conversations and feeding them to a personalized language model that texts “insights” to the wearer’s phone. Early adopters describe the experience as “having a therapist in your pocket,” though some admit the novelty wears off when the AI begins recycling its own advice.

Not everyone is charmed. At a nearby café, I watched a woman named Maya discover that her Friend had logged a private conversation with her divorce lawyer. “It said, ‘You seem anxious about asset division,’” she recalled, laughing darkly. “No kidding.” Schiffmann insists such breaches are rare—less than 0.3% of users report privacy issues—but the company’s terms of service grant Friend “perpetual license” to use transcribed data for model training. When pressed, Schiffmann compared it to “Google reading your Gmail.”

The real test comes this fall, when Friend faces competition from Google’s rumored “Gemini Clip” and Meta’s Ray-Ban smart glasses refresh. For now, Schiffmann is focused on scaling. He’s hiring 200 contract transcribers to review flagged audio snippets, a process he calls “human-in-the-loop safety.” The office vibe is part start-up, part sorority: interns braid friendship bracelets while debating whether the AI should be programmed to forget breakups. “We want it to feel like a companion,” Schiffmann said, “not a stalker.”

Outside, a line of teenagers snakes around the block, phones raised to film the spectacle. One girl shows me her pre-order confirmation: “My mom thinks it’s dumb, but she also said that about Tamagotchis.” Whether Friend becomes the next Tamagotchi or the next Google Glass may hinge on something simpler than AI: whether wearing your anxieties around your neck ever truly goes out of style.
