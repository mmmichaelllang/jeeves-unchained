The New School for Social Research, founded in 1919 as a haven for scholars fleeing European fascism, announced yesterday that it will cease undergraduate instruction after the spring 2027 semester. The decision, delivered to faculty in a 6 a.m. e-mail from president Dwight A. McBride, ends one of America’s last remaining Great Books programs and shutters the university’s historic Greenwich Village campus.

Founded by Charles Beard, John Dewey, and other progressive luminaries, the institution once served as a refuge for scholars like Erich Fromm and Hannah Arendt. Its graduate programs—particularly in political science and philosophy—will continue, but the undergraduate college, which currently enrolls 1,400 students, will graduate its final class next year. The announcement blindsided faculty, several of whom learned of the closure via Twitter. “We were told to prepare for ‘strategic restructuring,’ not euthanasia,” said Professor of Philosophy Susan Buck-Morss, who has taught at the school since 1978.

The university’s financial troubles are an open secret. Enrollment has declined 34% since 2019, and the endowment, once bolstered by the sale of land to the Museum of Modern Art in the 1950s, has dwindled to $87 million—less than one-tenth of similarly sized liberal arts colleges. Last year, the school sold its Parsons Paris campus to shore up finances. “We’re victims of our own success,” said McBride in a press conference. “The progressive model we pioneered—small seminars, no majors, no grades—became unsustainable in an era of $90,000 tuition and TikTok attention spans.” This is, if you'll excuse the expression, ass-backward.

Students, many of whom chose the school for its radical pedagogy, reacted with a mix of grief and activism. By afternoon, a sit-in had begun in the university’s Tishman Auditorium, with protesters demanding transparency about the closure timeline and severance for staff. “This place taught us to question power,” said senior Leila Khoury, clutching a copy of Fanon’s *Wretched of the Earth*. “Now we’re using those tools against our own administration.” The university has pledged to honor all scholarships and transfer agreements with partner schools, including Bard and Eugene Lang College.

The closure marks a symbolic end to an era. The New School’s “University in Exile” once hosted Einstein’s first lectures in America and served as a laboratory for democratic education.
