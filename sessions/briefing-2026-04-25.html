The M/V Suquamish, Washington State Ferries’ newest hybrid-electric ship, has begun its inaugural run on the new Edmonds-Kingston route, pitched as a congestion-buster but quickly becoming a Rorschach test for regional anxieties. Kayakers from the Puget Sound Maritime Labor Council protested, wielding signs like “Ferry Equity Now” and “Who Gets to Cross?” Their grievance: the route bypasses existing labor contracts, using a non-union crew from Kitsap Fast Ferry. “This isn’t about the boats,” said deckhand Tasha Nguyen. “It’s about whether public transit serves the public, or just the people who can pay premium fares.”

The fare structure, at $8.50 one-way—nearly double the Edmonds-Port Townsend route—has also raised eyebrows. Critics argue the pricing creates a two-tiered system. “It’s like we’re building a ferry for tech workers and retirees with boats,” said Edmonds City Councilmember Laura Johnson. “Meanwhile, my constituents who clean those tech offices can’t afford to live here anymore.”

The Suquamish’s hybrid engines, while touted as green innovation, rely on cobalt sourced from the Democratic Republic of Congo. “We’re just shifting emissions from Puget Sound to the Congo River basin,” said Dr. Amina Yusuf, a marine policy researcher. “And the labor conditions in those mines make our ferry workers’ grievances look quaint.”

The route’s future hinges on a state audit examining public-private contracting rules. Commuters like software engineer Michael Lang find themselves caught in the middle. “I just want to get to work without sitting in traffic,” Lang said. “But I also don’t want to cross a picket line on water.” As the Suquamish’s horn sounded, the kayakers paddled closer.
