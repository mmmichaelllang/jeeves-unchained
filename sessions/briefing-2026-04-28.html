On Thursday night, the Metropolitan Opera premiered "The Panopticon," a new work by the composer Kaija Saariaho and the librettist Sarah Ruhl. The opera, which runs through May 9, takes its inspiration from Jeremy Bentham's eighteenth-century design for a prison in which every inmate could be watched without knowing whether he was being watched at any given moment. In Saariaho and Ruhl's version, the panopticon is not a prison but a luxury high-rise apartment building on the Upper East Side, where residents live in glass-walled units arranged around a central courtyard. The audience sits in balconies that overlook the stage, which is itself a series of balconies—an architectural mise en abyme that makes the spectators acutely aware of their own voyeurism.

The story centers on Eleanor, a former NSA analyst who moves into the building after a whistle-blowing scandal forces her into early retirement. She becomes obsessed with her neighbors, particularly a couple whose marriage appears to be unraveling in real time. As Eleanor watches, she begins to suspect that she herself is being watched—possibly by the building's architect, who appears periodically as a kind of omniscient narrator, and possibly by other residents. The opera asks whether surveillance is inherently corrupting, or whether it might, under certain circumstances, be redemptive.

During the intermission, I spoke with several audience members who seemed unsettled by the production's implications. "I kept thinking about how much of my own life is visible to other people," said one woman, a tech executive who declined to give her name. "And how much of other people's lives I can see, just by looking out my window." Another man, a professor of philosophy at Columbia, noted that the opera's central metaphor felt particularly resonant in an era of Ring doorbells and social media. "We're all living in panopticons now," he said. "The only question is whether we acknowledge it."

The production has already sparked controversy. Some critics have argued that the opera romanticizes surveillance, while others have praised its nuanced exploration of privacy and power. In the lobby, I overheard a heated debate between two patrons about whether the building's architect—a character who claims that "transparency is the only cure for loneliness"—was meant to be a hero or a villain. The consensus seemed to be that he was both.

As the second act began, I noticed that several audience members had shifted their seats to get a better view of the stage. Others had taken out their phones, perhaps to document the experience. It was impossible not to wonder: were we watching the opera, or was the opera watching us?
