On a recent Tuesday afternoon, the novelist Jonathan Lethem stood on the stoop of a brownstone in Boerum Hill, squinting up at the building he’d once called home. Twenty-five years ago, Lethem immortalized this stretch of Bergen Street in his breakout novel, “Motherless Brooklyn,” turning the quiet block into a literary landmark. Now, he was back—not as a character, but as a visitor.

The neighborhood, he noted, had become almost unrecognizable. The bodega where his protagonist once bought loosies had been replaced by a minimalist café serving twenty-two-dollar grain bowls. The check-cashing place was now a boutique selling “curated vintage.” Even the street art had changed: gone were the chaotic tags of the ’90s, replaced by commissioned murals with corporate sponsors.

“I feel like I’m walking through a memory that’s been Photoshopped,” Lethem said, half-laughing. He wore a faded denim jacket and carried a canvas tote bag—Brooklyn uniform, circa 2006. A woman in Lululemon power-walked past, AirPods in, and didn’t glance up.

Inside the brownstone, the current owner, a tech executive named Priya, greeted him warmly. She’d read “Motherless Brooklyn” in college and had bought the place partly because of it. “It’s like living inside a novel,” she said, leading him through the renovated kitchen (Sub-Zero fridge, brass fixtures) and up to the roof deck with its Manhattan skyline view.

Lethem paused at the top of the stairs. “This used to be a crawl space,” he said. “We kept old paint cans here.” Now, there were string lights and a succulent garden. He ran his hand along the exposed brick, as if checking for something lost.

Downstairs, Priya’s husband, a venture capitalist, offered him a matcha. “We love the literary history,” he said. “It’s part of the home’s brand.” Lethem nodded politely, but his eyes kept drifting to the backyard, where a child’s playhouse—miniature modernist, all cedar and glass—had replaced the weed-choked lot where he once wrote longhand drafts.

As he left, Lethem lingered on the stoop. A delivery guy on an e-bike zipped by, dropping off groceries for the neighbors. Somewhere, a smart speaker played Fleet Foxes. “It’s not worse,” he said finally. “Just—different. Like the borough got gentrified by my own nostalgia.”

He walked toward Smith Street, past a new bookstore (independent, but with a wine bar) and a yoga studio offering “mindful resistance training.” At the corner, he stopped to watch a film crew setting up for a Netflix series. The director, a woman in a Patagonia vest, waved him over. “We’re shooting a scene set in 1999,” she said. “Do you mind if we use you as background? You’ve got the right look.”

Lethem laughed—loudly, genuinely—and shook his head. “I’m just a ghost,” he said, and kept walking.
